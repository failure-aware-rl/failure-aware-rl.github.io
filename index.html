Failure-Aware RL: Reliable Offline-to-Online Reinforcement Learning with Self-Recovery for Real-World Manipulation

Abstract

Post-training algorithms based on deep reinforcement learning can push the limits of robotic models for specific objectives, such as generalizability, accuracy, and robustness. However, intervention-requiring failures (e.g., a robot spilling water or breaking fragile glass) during real-world exploration happen inevitably, hindering the practical deployment of such a paradigm. To tackle this, we introduce Failure-Free Offline-to-Online Reinforcement Learning (FARL), a new paradigm minimizing failures during real-world reinforcement learning. We create FailureBench, a benchmark that incorporates common failure scenarios requiring human intervention, and propose an algorithm that integrates a world-model-based safety critic and a recovery policy trained offline to prevent failures during online exploration. Extensive simulation and real-world experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of FARL in significantly reducing such failures while improving performance and generalization during online reinforcement learning post-training. FARL reduces intervention-requiring failures by 73.1% while elevating performance by 11.3% on average during real-world RL post-training. Code of FARL and FailureBench will be publicly available.

Method

FARL is a failure-aware offline-to-online reinforcement learning framework. In the offline phase, we pre-train three components: a task policy \( \pi_{\text{task}} \), a recovery policy \( \pi_{\text{rec}} \), and a world model. The task and recovery policies are first trained by behavior cloning on task or recovery demonstrations and then offline-finetuned with a PPO-style objective. The world model is trained on both task and failure demonstrations to predict short-horizon rewards, values, and a constraint signal that captures near-future intervention-requiring failures.

In the online phase, we keep \( \pi_{\text{rec}} \) and the world model fixed and only fine-tune \( \pi_{\text{task}} \) in the real world. For each state, the world model rolls out \( \pi_{\text{task}} \) for a short horizon and estimates a discounted near-future constraint cost \( C_H^{\pi_{\text{task}}} \). If \( C_H^{\pi_{\text{task}}} \le \varepsilon_{\text{safe}} \), we execute the task action and record a task transition; otherwise we execute the recovery action and record a recovery transition. The task policy is then updated with the same PPO objective using advantages computed from these safety-filtered transitions, which improves performance while keeping intervention-requiring failures low.
